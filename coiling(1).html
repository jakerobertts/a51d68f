Coiling, Packing, Telomeres & Centromeres

  • DNA is organized into a compact structure through coiling and packing, which is essential for fitting long DNA molecules into the cell nucleus. The key components include:
    • Nucleosomes, which are fundamental units of DNA packaging, consisting of DNA wrapped around histone proteins.
    • Chromatin, which is the complex of DNA and proteins that forms chromosomes during cell division.
    • Telomeres, which are repetitive nucleotide sequences at the ends of chromosomes that protect them from degradation and prevent fusion with other chromosomes. Telomerase is an enzyme that extends telomeres, allowing cells to divide without losing important genetic information.
    • Centromeres, which are specialized regions of chromosomes that play a crucial role in chromosome segregation during cell division by serving as attachment points for spindle fibers.
    • Heterochromatin is tightly packed and transcriptionally inactive, while euchromatin is loosely packed and transcriptionally active.
    • Supercoiling is the twisting of DNA strands around each other, which can be either (overwound) or (underwound). It is important for DNA stability and regulation of gene expression. Topoisomerases are enzymes that manage DNA supercoiling by cutting and rejoining DNA strands to relieve torsional strain during DNA replication and transcription.
