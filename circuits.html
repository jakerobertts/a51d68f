1. Charges in an electric circuit are conserved. Which of the following is NOT true regarding charge conservation?
Individual charges cannot be created or destroyed






Charge is conserved in an electric system. Although the transfer of electrons can cause a charge change, this does not apply to fundamental units of charge. They are discrete and cannot change in value.
A. This is true. A closed system still has to maintain the same amount of charge, because there is no outside source to add or remove the charge.
B. This is true. Although charges may be distributed between conductors and insulators, an insulator does not allow for the movement of electrons within it, which means that the charge cannot be transferred through it.
C. This is true. The net charge of an isolated system is constant because there is no outside source to add or remove the charge.

2. Which of the following is not an example of an insulator?
A material with low resistivity






An insulator is a material that does not allow for free movement of electrons. This is often due to high resistivity, which is a measure of how strongly a material opposes the flow of electric current.

A. This is true. A dielectric is a type of insulator that can be polarized by an electric field, which allows it to store charge in a capacitor.
B. This is true. A material with physical limits that allow for conduction can still be an insulator if there is a high enough potential difference to cause electrical breakdown.
C. This is false. A material with low resistivity is a conductor, which allows for the free movement of electrons.
D. This is true. A material that does not allow for free movement of electrons is an insulator, and it must have high resistivity.

5. Which of the following is true about electric fields?
If a charge is twice as far from the source charge, the electric field strength is quartered






The electric field follows the inverse square law. If a charge is moved to twice the distance from the source charge, the electric field strength is reduced to one-fourth of its original value.

A. False. The direction of the electric field always ORIGINATES from a positive charge and TERMINATES at a negative charge.

6. Suppose you were trying to find the resistance of a material but were not given the current or voltage. Which of the following would decrease the resistance?
Increasing the cross-sectional area






The formula for resistance is: \[ R = \rho \frac{L}{A} \] where R is resistance, ρ is resistivity, L is length, and A is cross-sectional area. Resistance is inversely proportional to cross-sectional area, so increasing A decreases R.

7. Which of the following would increase the ability of a circuit to store charge?
Increasing the surface area of the plates in a capacitor






Capacitance is given by: \[ C = \kappa \epsilon_0 \frac{A}{d} \] Capacitance is directly proportional to surface area A, so increasing A increases the ability to store charge.

8. Which of the following would NOT be a characteristic of a conductor?
High ionization energy of an atom in the conductor






Conductors have low ionization energy, allowing electrons to move freely. High ionization energy would make it difficult to remove electrons, which is not characteristic of conductors.

9. A voltmeter is used to measure the potential difference across two points in a circuit. What can you most likely expect in terms of its properties?
It has a very high resistance






A voltmeter has very high resistance to minimize current flow through it, ensuring it doesn't significantly affect the circuit being measured. It connects in parallel, not series.

10. Which of the following is true about current in a circuit?
Current can be negative






Current can be negative, indicating flow in the opposite direction to the assumed positive charge flow. Current is measured in amperes, not volts. Conventional current assumes positive charge flow, even though electrons (negative) actually move.
