Mitosis & Meiosis Overview

  • Mitosis is the process of cell division that results in two identical daughter cells, each with the same number of chromosomes as the parent cell.
  • Mitosis and meiosis both involve the replication of DNA and the separation of chromosomes.
  • Meiosis is a specialized form of cell division that reduces the chromosome number by half, resulting in four non-identical gametes (sperm or eggs) with half the number of chromosomes.
    • During meiosis, homologous chromosomes are separated into different gametes, ensuring genetic diversity.
    • Crossing over can occur during prophase I of meiosis, allowing for the exchange of genetic material between homologous chromosomes.
      • Single crossover involves the exchange of genetic material between two homologous chromosomes at one location.
      • Double crossover involves two crossover events, where segments of genetic material are exchanged at two locations on homologous chromosomes.
    • The synaptonemal complex forms during prophase I of meiosis, facilitating the pairing of homologous chromosomes and the exchange of genetic material.
    • A tetrad is a structure formed during meiosis when homologous chromosomes pair up, consisting of four chromatids (two from each homologous chromosome).
