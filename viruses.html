Viruses & Other Infectious Agents

Viral Structure and Classification

  • A Capsid is the protein shell that surrounds and protects the viral genetic material.
  • An Envelope is a lipid membrane that surrounds some viruses, derived from host cell membranes.
  • Bacteriophages are viruses that specifically infect bacterial cells.
  • Retroviruses are RNA viruses that use reverse transcriptase to integrate into the host genome.

    • Viral Replication Cycles

  • The lytic cycle results in immediate reproduction of the virus and destruction of the host cell.
  • The lysogenic cycle involves integration of viral DNA into the host chromosome, remaining dormant.
  • Provirus is viral DNA that has been integrated into the host cell's chromosome.
  • Viral load refers to the concentration of virus particles in a given sample.

    • Viral Proteins and Enzymes

  • Reverse transcriptase is an enzyme that synthesizes DNA from an RNA template.
  • Integrase is an enzyme that inserts viral DNA into the host cell's chromosome.
  • Protease is an enzyme that cleaves viral proteins into functional components.
  • Hemagglutinin is a surface protein that allows viruses to attach to and enter host cells.

    • Other Infectious Agents

  • Prions are infectious protein particles that lack nucleic acid and cause neurodegenerative diseases.
  • Viroids are small, circular RNA molecules that infect plants without a protein coat.
  • Transposons are DNA sequences that can move from one location to another within a genome.
  • Plasmids are self-replicating genetic elements that exist independently of chromosomes.

    • Host-Pathogen Interactions

  • Host range refers to the spectrum of host species that a virus can infect.
  • Viral tropism is the specificity of viruses for particular cell types or tissues.
  • Latency is a state where the virus remains dormant in the host without active replication.
  • Antigenic drift is the gradual accumulation of mutations in viral surface proteins over time.
