\[ P + \frac{1}{2}\rho v^2 + \rho gh = \text{constant} \]
Where P = pressure, ρ = density, v = velocity, g = gravity, h = height

Key Relationship:
\[ P_1 + \frac{1}{2}\rho v_1^2 = P_2 + \frac{1}{2}\rho v_2^2 \]
(assuming constant height)


A. Incorrect - This violates Bernoulli's principle; velocity and pressure are inversely related

Real-world examples:

Reynolds Number (Re):

The Reynolds number is a dimensionless quantity used to predict flow patterns in different fluid flow situations. It is defined as the ratio of inertial forces to viscous forces and is given by the formula:
Re = (ρ * v * L) / μ
Where:
- ρ is the fluid density
- v is the flow velocity
- L is a characteristic length (e.g., diameter of a pipe)
- μ is the dynamic viscosity of the fluid
A Reynolds number less than 2000 typically indicates laminar flow, while a Reynolds number greater than 4000 indicates turbulent flow. Transitional flow occurs between these two values. Blood from the left ventricle to the aorta has a high velocity and larger diameter, leading to a Reynolds number greater than 2000, indicating turbulent flow.

____________________________________________________________________________ 11. When conducting hemoglobin a1C tests, blood enters and climbs the tube easily before being analyzed by a machine. Why would the blood be able to climb the tube easily?

Capillary Action:

Capillary action is the ability of a liquid to flow in narrow spaces without the assistance of external forces (like gravity). This phenomenon occurs due to the adhesive forces between the liquid and the surface of the material, as well as the cohesive forces within the liquid itself.



Key Factors:
1. Adhesion: The attraction between the liquid molecules and the surface of the tube. If the adhesive forces are stronger than the cohesive forces, the liquid will climb the walls of the tube.
2. Cohesion: The attraction between the liquid molecules themselves. Strong cohesive forces can resist the upward movement of the liquid.

From arteries to arterioles






"It's like going from a highway to a narrow street in one go."

Blood Pressure Drop in Circulatory System:

Blood pressure decreases as blood flows through the circulatory system, primarily due to the resistance encountered in smaller vessels. The most significant drop in blood pressure occurs when blood moves from arteries to arterioles. This is because arterioles have a much smaller diameter compared to arteries, leading to increased resistance and a consequent drop in pressure.

Key Points:
1. Arteries: These are large vessels that carry blood away from the heart under high pressure.
2. Arterioles: These are smaller branches of arteries that lead to capillaries. The significant reduction in diameter causes a major drop in blood pressure.
3. Capillaries: These are tiny vessels where exchange of gases and nutrients occurs. The pressure continues to drop but not as drastically as in arterioles.
4. Venules and Veins: These vessels carry blood back to the heart under lower pressure.

15. According to the venturi effect, what happens to the pressure of a fluid as it flows through a constricted section of a pipe?

Venturi Effect:

The Venturi effect describes the reduction in static pressure - pressure that is exerted by a fluid at rest - that occurs when a fluid flows through a constricted section of a pipe. This phenomenon is a direct consequence of the conservation of energy in fluid dynamics, as described by Bernoulli's principle.

Mathematical Representation:
Bernoulli's equation can be expressed as:
\[ P_1 + \frac{1}{2}\rho v_1^2 = P_2 + \frac{1}{2}\rho v_2^2 \]
Where:
- \( P_1 \) and \( P_2 \) are the static pressures at points 1 and 2
- \( v_1 \) and \( v_2 \) are the fluid velocities at points 1 and 2
- \( \rho \) is the fluid density
