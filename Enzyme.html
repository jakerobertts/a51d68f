Enzyme function

  • Enzymes are biological catalysts that speed up chemical reactions by lowering the activation energy required for the reaction to occur.
  • Enzymes are highly specific, meaning they only catalyze specific reactions or act on specific substrates.
  • The active site of an enzyme is the region where the substrate binds, and it is typically a small pocket or groove on the enzyme's surface.
  • Enzyme activity can be affected by factors such as temperature, pH, and substrate concentration.

Enzyme Kinetics

  • Enzyme kinetics is the study of the rates of enzyme-catalyzed reactions and how they change in response to various factors.
  • The mechanism of catalysis involves the formation of an enzyme-substrate complex, which is a transient complex formed when the enzyme binds to its substrate.
  • The Michaelis-Menten equation describes the relationship between the rate of an enzyme-catalyzed reaction and the concentration of substrate.
  • The Vmax is the maximum rate of reaction when the enzyme is saturated with substrate.
  • The Km is the substrate concentration at which the reaction rate is half of Vmax.
  • The kcat is the turnover number, giving the rate of reaction per unit enzyme concentration.

    • Enzyme Cooperativity

  • The cooperativity of an enzyme refers to the way its activity changes in response to substrate binding.
  • Enzymes can exhibit positive cooperativity, where the binding of one substrate molecule increases the enzyme's affinity for additional substrate molecules.
  • Alternatively, they can exhibit negative cooperativity, where the binding of one substrate molecule decreases the enzyme's affinity for additional substrate molecules.
  • Cooperativity can be visualized using a sigmoidal curve, which indicates that the enzyme's activity increases more rapidly at certain substrate concentrations.
  • Cooperativity is measured by the Hill coefficient, which quantifies the degree of cooperativity in enzyme activity.

    • Enzyme Inhibition

  • Enzyme inhibitors can be classified as competitive, non-competitive, or uncompetitive based on their mechanism of action.
  • Competitive inhibitors bind to the active site of the enzyme, preventing substrate binding.
  • Non-competitive inhibitors reduce the enzyme's activity without affecting substrate binding.
  • Uncompetitive inhibitors bind to the enzyme-substrate complex, preventing the conversion of substrate to product.

    • Dive deeper: Although enzymes lower the activation energy of a reaction, they do not change the overall free energy change (ΔG) of the reaction. This means that while enzymes can speed up the rate of a reaction, they cannot make an unfavorable reaction favorable.Therefore, the equilibrium constant (Keq) remains unchanged in the presence of an enzyme.
