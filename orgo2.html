Passage 1 (Questions 1‑5)

Amino acids are the fundamental building blocks of proteins and contain both amino (-NH₂) and carboxyl (-COOH) functional groups. These organic compounds exist as zwitterions at physiological pH, with the amino group protonated and the carboxyl group deprotonated. The twenty standard amino acids differ in their side chains (R-groups), which determine their chemical properties and biological functions.

Peptide bonds form through condensation reactions between the carboxyl group of one amino acid and the amino group of another, creating an amide linkage with partial double-bond character due to resonance. This restricts rotation around the C-N bond and gives proteins their structural stability. All naturally occurring amino acids in proteins (except glycine) have the L-configuration.

The isoelectric point (pI) of an amino acid is the pH at which it carries no net charge. At pH values below the pI, amino acids carry a net positive charge, while at pH values above the pI, they carry a net negative charge. This property is crucial for protein purification and analysis techniques.

Passage 2 (Questions 6‑10)

Amino acid synthesis is crucial for understanding biochemical pathways and pharmaceutical development. The Strecker synthesis and Gabriel synthesis are two important methods for preparing amino acids in the laboratory. Both reactions typically yield racemic mixtures of D- and L-amino acids, requiring additional steps for enantiomeric resolution if pure stereoisomers are needed.

The Strecker synthesis begins with an aldehyde, which reacts with ammonia or an amine to form an imine intermediate. This imine then undergoes nucleophilic addition with hydrogen cyanide to form an α-amino nitrile, which is subsequently hydrolyzed to yield the amino acid. The Gabriel synthesis utilizes potassium phthalimide, diethyl bromomalonate, and an alkyl halide in a multi-step process.

Aliphatic amino acids are characterized by hydrocarbon side chains containing carbon and hydrogen atoms joined in straight chains, branched chains, or non-aromatic rings. These amino acids contrast with aromatic amino acids, which contain benzene rings or other aromatic systems in their side chains.

Passage 3 (Questions 11‑15)

Phosphorus-containing compounds play essential roles in biological systems and organic chemistry. Phosphoric acid (H₃PO₄) is a triprotic acid with three ionizable hydrogens, each having distinct pKa values (pKa₁ = 2.1, pKa₂ = 7.2, pKa₃ = 12.3). This makes phosphoric acid an excellent biological buffer system, particularly around physiological pH.

At physiological pH (~7.4), phosphoric acid exists primarily as a mixture of H₂PO₄⁻ and HPO₄²⁻ ions, which can act as both proton donors and acceptors. The term "inorganic phosphate" (Pi) refers to these phosphate species. Organic phosphates, such as ATP and GTP, contain phosphate groups covalently bonded to organic molecules and serve as energy storage compounds.

The backbone of DNA consists of sugar molecules connected by phosphodiester bonds, which link the 3'-hydroxyl group of one sugar to the 5'-phosphate group of the next. These bonds are crucial for the structural integrity of nucleic acids and are formed through condensation reactions that eliminate water molecules.
