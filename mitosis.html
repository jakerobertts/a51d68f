Mitosis & Meiosis Overview

  • Mitosis is the process by which a single cell divides to produce two genetically identical daughter cells, each with the same number of chromosomes as the original cell (diploid, 2n).
  • Mitosis is essential for growth, repair, and asexual reproduction in multicellular organisms.
  • The stages of mitosis are: prophase, metaphase, anaphase, and telophase, followed by cytokinesis.
  • Meiosis is a two-part cell division process that produces four genetically unique gametes (sperm or egg cells), each with half the number of chromosomes of the original cell (haploid, n).
  • Meiosis introduces genetic diversity through crossing over (prophase I) and independent assortment (metaphase I).
  • The two divisions of meiosis are called meiosis I (homologous chromosomes separate) and meiosis II (sister chromatids separate).
  • Key differences: Mitosis produces two identical diploid cells for growth and repair; meiosis produces four unique haploid gametes for sexual reproduction.
  • Homologous chromosomes are pairs of chromosomes (one from each parent) that have the same genes but may have different alleles.
  • Sister chromatids are identical copies of a chromosome, connected by a centromere, separated during mitosis and meiosis II.
  • Synapsis and tetrad formation occur in prophase I of meiosis, allowing for crossing over.
  • Nondisjunction is the failure of chromosomes to separate properly during meiosis, leading to aneuploidy.

Dive deeper: In meiosis I, reduction division occurs, where homologous chromosomes are separated into different daughter cells, halving the chromosome number. Crossing over and independent assortment are exclusive to meiosis and contribute to genetic diversity in the resulting gametes. Crossing over increases genetic variation by producing recombinant chromosomes, while independent assortment contributes to genetic diversity by producing different combinations of alleles in gametes.
