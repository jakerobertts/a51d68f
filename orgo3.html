Passage 1 (Questions 1‑5)

Carbonyl compounds are organic molecules containing a carbon-oxygen double bond (C=O) and represent one of the most important functional groups in organic chemistry. The two primary types of simple carbonyl compounds are aldehydes and ketones, which differ in their structural arrangements and chemical properties.

Aldehydes contain a carbonyl group bonded to at least one hydrogen atom, making them terminal functional groups. The carbonyl carbon in aldehydes is sp² hybridized and exhibits partial positive character due to the electronegativity difference between carbon and oxygen. This electrophilic nature makes the carbonyl carbon susceptible to nucleophilic attack.

Ketones have their carbonyl carbon bonded to two other carbon atoms, placing the carbonyl group within the carbon skeleton rather than at a terminus. Both aldehydes and ketones can exist in equilibrium with their enol forms through keto-enol tautomerization, though the keto form typically predominates due to the higher stability of the C=O double bond compared to the C=C double bond.

Passage 2 (Questions 5‑8)

The synthesis and reduction of carbonyl compounds involves selective oxidation and reduction reactions that must be carefully controlled to achieve the desired products. Primary alcohols can be oxidized to aldehydes or further to carboxylic acids, while secondary alcohols are oxidized to ketones. The choice of oxidizing agent determines the extent of oxidation.

Weak oxidizing agents such as pyridinium chlorochromate (PCC) allow for the controlled oxidation of primary alcohols to aldehydes without further oxidation to carboxylic acids. Strong oxidizing agents like potassium permanganate (KMnO₄) and chromic acid (H₂Cr₂O₄) will completely oxidize primary alcohols to carboxylic acids and secondary alcohols to ketones.

Reduction reactions of carbonyl compounds require careful selection of reducing agents based on the desired outcome. Sodium borohydride (NaBH₄) is a mild reducing agent capable of reducing aldehydes and ketones to alcohols but cannot reduce carboxylic acids or esters. For the reduction of carboxylic acids and esters to alcohols, stronger reducing agents such as lithium aluminum hydride (LiAlH₄) are required.

Passage 3 (Questions 9‑11)

Nucleophilic addition reactions to carbonyl compounds result in a variety of important organic products. These reactions occur when nucleophiles attack the electrophilic carbonyl carbon, leading to the formation of new carbon-heteroatom bonds. The specific products depend on the nature of the nucleophile and reaction conditions.

Hydration reactions involve the addition of water to carbonyl compounds, resulting in the formation of geminal diols (compounds with two hydroxyl groups on the same carbon). While most geminal diols are unstable and readily eliminate water to regenerate the carbonyl, some can be stabilized under specific conditions.

Alcohols can add to carbonyl compounds to form hemiacetals (from aldehydes) or hemiketals (from ketones) when one equivalent of alcohol is used. With two equivalents of alcohol, full acetals or ketals are formed. Nitrogen-containing nucleophiles such as amines react with carbonyls to form imines, which can undergo tautomerization to form enamines under appropriate conditions.

Passage 4 (Questions 12‑15)

The α-carbon in carbonyl compounds plays a crucial role in many important organic reactions. This carbon atom, adjacent to the carbonyl group, bears hydrogen atoms that are significantly more acidic than typical alkyl hydrogens due to the electron-withdrawing effect of the carbonyl group. Removal of an α-hydrogen results in the formation of an enolate anion, a key intermediate in many synthetic transformations.

Enolate formation can occur under kinetic or thermodynamic control, leading to different regioisomers when multiple α-carbons are present. Kinetic enolates form rapidly and typically involve deprotonation of the less hindered α-carbon, while thermodynamic enolates form under equilibrating conditions and favor the more substituted, more stable enolate.

The Aldol addition reaction represents one of the most powerful methods for carbon-carbon bond formation in organic synthesis. In this reaction, an enolate anion acts as a nucleophile and attacks the carbonyl carbon of another aldehyde or ketone. The Michael addition is another important reaction involving enolates, where an enolate attacks an α,β-unsaturated carbonyl compound to form a new carbon-carbon bond.
