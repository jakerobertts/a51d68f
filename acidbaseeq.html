The following questions are based on the passage below:


The carbonic acid buffer system is crucial in maintaining the pH of blood within a narrow range (approximately 7.35 to 7.45). This system involves the equilibrium between carbonic acid (H2CO3) and its conjugate base, bicarbonate (HCO3⁻). The relevant equilibrium reaction is as follows:

CO2 + H2O ⇌ H2CO3 ⇌ H+ + HCO3⁻

The pKa of carbonic acid is approximately 6.1. This means that at a pH of 6.1, the concentrations of H2CO3 and HCO3⁻ are equal. However, since the physiological pH of blood is around 7.4, the equilibrium lies significantly to the right, favoring the formation of bicarbonate ions. This allows the buffer system to effectively neutralize excess acids (which increase H+ concentration) or bases (which decrease H+ concentration) in the blood.

When an acid is introduced into the bloodstream, the excess H+ ions react with bicarbonate ions to form carbonic acid, which can then be converted to carbon dioxide (CO2) and water (H2O) through the action of the enzyme carbonic anhydrase. The CO2 is then exhaled through the lungs, helping to remove the excess acid from the body. Conversely, when a base is introduced, carbonic acid can dissociate to release H+ ions, thereby neutralizing the base and maintaining the pH balance.
