Sensory Receptors & Pathways

  • Sensory receptors are specialized cells that detect and respond to specific environmental stimuli such as light, sound, touch, chemicals, or temperature.
    • Types of sensory receptors: Include
    • mechanoreceptors
    • chemoreceptors
    • photoreceptors
    • thermoreceptors
    • nociceptors
  • Sensory pathways are neural routes that carry sensory information from receptors through the nervous system to processing centers in the brain.
    • Pathways: Include afferent pathways that transmit sensory information to the central nervous system (CNS) for processing.
    • efferent pathways that transmit motor commands from the central nervous system (CNS) to effectors (muscles and glands).

    Eye & Vision (Biological Aspects)

    • Structure and function of the eye:
      • Cornea: focuses light
      • Lens: adjusts focus
      • Retina: contains photoreceptors
      • Optic nerve: transmits visual information to brain

    Ear & Hearing (Biological Aspects)

    • Structure and function of the ear:
      • Outer ear: collects sound
      • Middle ear: amplifies vibrations
      • Inner ear: converts vibrations to neural signals
      • Vestibular system: maintains balance
        • Semicircular canals: detect rotational movement
        • Otolith organs: detect linear acceleration
    • Hair cells are specialized mechanoreceptors in the cochlea that convert mechanical vibrations into electrical signals through deflection of stereocilia, enabling hearing.

    Other Senses (Biological Aspects)

    • Somatosensation is the body's sense of touch, pressure, temperature, and pain perception through specialized receptors in skin, muscles, and internal organs.
      • Proprioception is the sense of body position and movement, mediated by proprioceptors in muscles, tendons, and joints.
      • Nociception is the sensory process that detects pain through nociceptors, specialized receptors that respond to potentially damaging stimuli.
      • Thermoreception is the ability to sense temperature changes through thermoreceptors in the skin and other tissues.
    • Taste is chemoreception by taste buds containing receptor cells that detect five basic tastes: sweet, sour, salty, bitter, and umami (savory).
      • Fungiform papillae are mushroom-shaped structures on the tongue that contain taste buds, primarily responsible for detecting sweet and salty tastes.
      • Foliate papillae are folds on the sides of the tongue that contain taste buds, primarily responsible for detecting sour tastes.
      • Circumvallate papillae are large, dome-shaped structures at the back of the tongue that contain taste buds, primarily responsible for detecting bitter tastes.
    • Smell is chemoreception by olfactory cells in the nasal cavity that detect airborne chemical molecules and send signals directly to the olfactory bulb in the brain.
    • Pheromones are chemical signals released by organisms that trigger behavioral or physiological responses in other members of the same species, important in reproduction and social behavior.
    • Olfactory pathways in the brain:
      • Olfactory receptors in the nasal cavity send signals to the olfactory bulb
      • Signals are processed in the olfactory cortex
      • Connects directly to the limbic system (emotion and memory centers)
      • Does not pass through the thalamus

    Neural Basis of Memory & Learning

    • Changes in synaptic connections underlie memory and learning: Memory formation involves strengthening or weakening synaptic connections through mechanisms:
      • Long-term potentiation is the strengthening of synapses based on recent patterns of activity.
      • Long-term depression is the weakening of synapses based on the absence or decrease of activity.
    • Neural plasticity is the brain's ability to reorganize and adapt by forming new neural connections, modifying existing ones, and even generating new neurons throughout life.

    Processes in Emotion

    • The role of biological processes in perceiving emotion: Emotions involve complex interactions between neurotransmitters:
      • Dopamine is the neurotransmitter associated with pleasure and reward, influencing motivation and mood.
      • Norepinephrine is involved in arousal and alertness, affecting the body's response to stress.
      • Serotonin is linked to mood regulation, and its imbalance is associated with depression and anxiety.
      and brain structures:
      • The Amygdala is involved in fear processing and emotional responses.
      • The Prefrontal Cortex is responsible for emotional regulation and decision-making.
      • The Hippocampus plays a role in emotional memory and context.
    • Regions involved in the generation and experience of emotions: Primarily components of the limbic system including amygdala, hippocampus, cingulate cortex, and associated structures.
    • The limbic system processes emotional responses, links emotions to memories, and coordinates emotional behavior with the autonomic nervous system.
    • Emotion and the autonomic nervous system:
      • The Sympathetic division triggers "fight-or-flight" responses.
      • The Parasympathetic division promotes "rest-and-digest" states.
    • Physiological markers of emotion: Measurable changes include heart rate, blood pressure, skin conductance, cortisol levels, and facial muscle activity.
    • Physiological Response to Stress: Activation of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis leading to release of stress hormones like cortisol and adrenaline.

    Nervous System

    • The nervous system is divided into
      • Central nervous system (brain and spinal cord)
      • Peripheral nervous system (all nerves outside).
      which are responsible for controlling and coordinating body functions.
    • Neurons: Nerve cells consist of:
      • Dendrites (receive signals)
      • Cell body/soma (processes signals)
      • Axon (transmits signals)
      forming the basic functional unit of the nervous system.
    • Neurotransmitters are chemical messengers that transmit signals across synapses between neurons, including:
      • Acetylcholine
      • Dopamine
      • Serotonin
      • GABA
      • Glutamate
    • Structure and function of the peripheral nervous system:
      • Somatic nervous system (voluntary motor control)
      • Autonomic nervous system (involuntary functions like heart rate, digestion).
    • Structure and function of the central nervous system:
      • Brain (processing center for sensory information, emotions, cognition)
      • Spinal cord (transmits signals between brain and body, coordinates reflexes).

    The brain

    • Forebrain:
      • The Cerebrum is involved in higher cognitive functions, and consciousness
      • The Thalamus is the sensory relay center
      • The Hypothalamus is responsible for homeostasis and hormone regulation.
    • Midbrain:
      • Structures involved in visual and auditory reflexes, eye movement, and parts of the reticular formation that regulate arousal and consciousness.
    • Hindbrain: Includes the:
      • Cerebellum (coordination, balance)
      • Pons (sleep, arousal, facial sensation)
      • Medulla oblongata (vital functions like breathing, heart rate)
    • Lateralization of cortical functions: Specialization of brain hemispheres:
      • The left hemisphere is typically associated with language, analytical thinking, and logical reasoning.
      • The right hemisphere is associated with spatial processing, creativity, and holistic thinking.
    • Methods used in studying the brain:
      • Functional MRI which measures brain activity by detecting changes in blood flow.
      • PET scans which use radioactive tracers to visualize brain activity.
      • EEG which records electrical activity of the brain through electrodes on the scalp.
      • CT scans which provide detailed images of brain structure using X-rays.
      • Lesion studies which involve examining the effects of brain damage on behavior.
      • Electrophysiology which measures electrical activity in the brain using electrodes.

    The spinal cord

    • Structure and function of the spinal cord:
      • Gray matter (cell bodies) forms the central butterfly shape.
      • White matter (myelinated axons) surrounds it, organizing ascending and descending pathways for sensory and motor information.
    • Spinal reflexes are automatic responses that occur at the spinal cord level without brain involvement and include:
      • Stretch reflex (e.g., knee-jerk reflex)
      • Withdrawal reflex (e.g., pulling back from a painful stimulus)

    The endocrine system

    • Components of the endocrine system:
      • Hypothalamus (regulates pituitary gland through the HPA axis)
      • Pituitary gland (master gland)
      • Thyroid gland (metabolism)
      • Adrenal glands (stress response)
      • Pancreas (blood sugar)
      • Gonads (reproduction)
    • Effects of the endocrine system on behavior: There are several hormones that influence behavior, including:
      • Adrenaline (increases arousal and energy)
      • Cortisol (regulates stress response)
      • Oxytocin (promotes bonding and social behaviors)
      • Testosterone (associated with aggression and dominance)
      • Estrogen (involved in reproductive functions and mood regulation)
      • Melatonin (regulates sleep-wake cycles)

    Behavioral Genetics

    • Genes, temperament, and heredity: Genetic variations influence personality traits, behavioral tendencies, and predispositions to certain behaviors through inherited DNA sequences.
    • Adaptive value of traits and behaviors: Behaviors and traits that enhance survival and reproductive success are favored by natural selection and become more common in populations.
    • Interaction between heredity and environmental influences: Gene-environment interactions determine how genetic predispositions are expressed, with environment modulating genetic effects.
    • Regulatory genes and behavior: Genes that control the expression of other genes, influencing when and where behavioral programs are activated during development and in response to stimuli.
    • Genetically based behavioral variation in natural populations: Genetic diversity within species creates behavioral differences that may be adaptive in different environments or circumstances.

    Human Physiological Development

    • Prenatal development: Development from fertilization to birth, including embryonic stage (organ formation) and fetal stage (growth and maturation), with critical periods for normal development.
      • Fertilization: The union of sperm and egg
        • Acrosome reaction: Enzymes from the sperm penetrate the egg's outer layer
        • Cortical reaction: Egg releases enzymes to prevent multiple sperm from entering
        • Zygote: The fertilized egg that undergoes cell division
        • Blastocyst: A hollow ball of cells that implants in the uterine wall
      • Embryonic stage: Major organs and structures form (weeks 3-8)
        • Neural tube forms (week 3)
        • Heart begins to beat (week 4)
        • Limbs and facial features develop (weeks 5-8)
      • Fetal stage: Growth and maturation of organs (weeks 9-40)
    • Motor development: Progressive acquisition of movement skills from basic reflexes in infancy to complex coordinated movements, following predictable developmental sequences.
      • Reflexes (e.g., grasping, rooting) are present at birth.
      • Gross motor skills (e.g., crawling, walking) develop in early childhood.
      • Fine motor skills (e.g., grasping small objects, writing) develop later in childhood.
    • Developmental changes in adolescence: Puberty triggers hormonal changes, physical growth spurts, sexual maturation, and continued brain development, especially prefrontal cortex maturation.
      • Puberty: The period during which adolescents reach sexual maturity and become capable of reproduction.

    Biological Bases of Disorders & Therapies

    • Biological bases of nervous system disorders (Schizophrenia, Depression, Alzheimer’s disease, Parkinson’s disease)
      • Neurotransmitter imbalances: Abnormal levels of neurotransmitters (e.g., dopamine, serotonin) are implicated in these disorders.
      • Genetic factors: Hereditary components contribute to the risk of developing these conditions.
      • Brain structure abnormalities: Differences in brain anatomy (e.g., enlarged ventricles in schizophrenia) are observed.
    • Stem cell-based therapy to regenerate neurons in the central nervous system:
      • Stem cells can differentiate into various cell types, including neurons, offering potential for repairing damaged brain tissue.
      • Research is ongoing to develop effective methods for delivering stem cells to the brain and ensuring their survival and integration into existing neural networks.

    Biological Processes in Motivation

    • Drives: Biological needs that create internal tension and motivate behavior to restore homeostasis, operating through negative feedback systems (e.g., hunger drive when blood glucose drops).
    • Incentives: External stimuli or rewards that motivate behavior by providing positive reinforcement (e.g., food, social approval).
    • Biological and sociocultural motivators that regulate behavior:
      • Hunger involves hypothalamic regulation and hormones:
        • Ghrelin stimulates appetite
        • Leptin signals satiety
      • Sex drive involves hormones and brain reward systems;
      • Addiction involves dopamine pathways and neuroplasticity.

    Biological Processes in Learning

    • Classical conditioning (Biological aspects):
        Involves synaptic changes in brain circuits that link conditioned and unconditioned stimuli, with the amygdala playing key roles in fear conditioning.
      • Operant conditioning (Biological aspects): Relies on brain reward systems that strengthen behaviors followed by positive outcomes and weaken those followed by negative outcomes.
      • Biological processes that affect associative learning: Biological predispositions make some associations easier to learn:
        • Taste aversion is a learned avoidance of a particular food that has been associated with illness.
        • Preparedness refers to the innate predisposition to learn certain associations more easily than others, such as associating snakes with fear.
        • Instinctive drift causes learned behaviors to revert to species-typical patterns.
      • Biological processes that affect observational learning:
        • Mirror neurons are specialized neurons that fire when an individual performs an action and when they observe the same action performed by others, facilitating imitation and learning.
        • Social learning involves observing and imitating others, with mirror neurons playing a key role in understanding and replicating behaviors.

    Animal Social Behavior (Biological Aspects)

    • Animal signals and communication:
      • Chemical signals known as pheromones
      • Visual displays:
        • Coloration (e.g., bright colors in birds)
        • Body language (e.g., tail position in dogs)
        • Mating displays (e.g., courtship rituals)
        • Threat displays (e.g., puffing up in pufferfish)
      • Auditory signals
        • Vocalizations (e.g., bird songs, wolf howls)
        • Calls (e.g., alarm calls in primates)
    • Biological explanations of social behavior in animals: Social behaviors have evolutionary origins and genetic bases, often serving functions like:
      • Mate selection: where individuals choose mates based on specific traits.
      • Territory defense involves behaviors that protect an area from intruders.
      • Predator avoidance includes strategies to evade or deter predators.
      • Cooperative resource acquisition refers to group behaviors that enhance foraging success.
    • Foraging behavior: Food-seeking strategies that balance energy costs and benefits, following optimal foraging theory to maximize energy gain while minimizing risks and energy expenditure.
    • Applying game theory: Mathematical models used to predict behavioral strategies in competitive and cooperative situations, helping explain phenomena like aggression, cooperation, and resource sharing.
    • Altruism: Behaviors that benefit others at a cost to the individual, explained through kin selection (helping relatives) and reciprocal altruism (helping others who may return the favor).
    • Inclusive fitness: An individual's total genetic contribution to future generations, including both personal reproduction and the reproduction of relatives (who share genes), explaining altruistic behaviors.
