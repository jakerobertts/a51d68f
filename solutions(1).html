Solutions

Solution Formation and Properties

  • Hydration occurs when water molecules surround and interact with dissolved ions or polar molecules.
  • Solubility is the maximum amount of solute that can dissolve in a given amount of solvent at a specific temperature.
  • A saturated solution contains the maximum amount of solute that can dissolve at equilibrium.
  • A supersaturated solution contains more dissolved solute than would normally be possible at that temperature.

Concentration Units

  • Molarity (M) is defined as moles of solute per liter of solution: M = mol solute / L solution
  • Concentration can be expressed in various units including molarity, molality, mass percent, and mole fraction.
  • Dilution calculations use the relationship M₁V₁ = M₂V₂ where M₁V₁ equals M₂V₂.

Solubility Equilibria

  • The Ksp (solubility product constant) quantifies the equilibrium between a solid and its dissolved ions.
  • The common-ion effect describes how the presence of a common ion decreases the solubility of a compound.
  • Complex ions form when metal cations coordinate with ligands, often increasing solubility.
  • pH effects on solubility are important for compounds containing basic anions or acidic cations.

Ionic Solutions and Hydronium

  • The hydronium ion (H₃O⁺) is formed when acids donate protons to water molecules.
  • In aqueous solutions, hydrogen ions (H⁺) exist as H₃O⁺ rather than free protons.
  • Ionic compounds dissolve through ion-dipole interactions with water molecules in the hydration process.

Solution Calculations

  • To calculate molarity: divide moles of solute by liters of solution.
  • For dilutions, the number of moles remains constant, so n₁ = n₂ in dilution problems.
  • When solutions mix, use conservation of mass and the equation MfVf = M₁V₁ + M₂V₂ for the final concentration.
